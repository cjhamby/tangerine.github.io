Key Holder

One day, the plastic housing that held my friend’s key fob together broke.

Key - Exploded View

The plastic shell had two halves. One was connected to the metal on the key, and the other snapped onto it. Inside the shell were the electronics responsible for locking/unlocking doors and allowing the car to start. Some tabs on the plastic shell had broken off, so the case couldn’t snap together anymore. Fortunately, it fell apart in their bag and all of the pieces were contained- replacing the electronics inside the key would’ve cost a lot. While a new case wouldn’t have been as expensive, I wanted to attempt to 3D print a “free” solution.

Finished Design

Design Process

Rather than try to print a replica of the shell piece, I designed a larger “container” that could hold the other pieces. This shell implemented the buttons (which had gone missing by this point), and prevented the other pieces from escaping.

Two types of 3D modeling are solid modeling and wireframe modeling 1. Solid modeling is good for designs that use simple geometric shapes, such as my loom project. I wanted the piece I printed to fit the form of the curved key, which would’ve been a difficult task in solid modeling. Wireframe modeling allows the user to create geometry based on sets of verteces.

I used Blender for the wireframe modeling. I tried to keep the design as lightweight and form-fitting as possible. Not many vertices were needed for prototyping- the printer resolution was one limiting factor. The geometry was traced from a reference photo. I marked where the buttons and locating tab were.

wireframe_trace

Once the mesh had the correct shape, it needed to be dimensioned. I opened the mesh in Fusion 3602 to achieve this. I set the scale by measuring the key at a couple of points with calipers. I then printed a test plate to ensure the geometry matched up.

test_fit_1

After successfully fitting the part, I added a few features. The first was a slot to hold the metal portion of the key. The metal was attached to the back half of the plastic. Holding the metal key also held the plastic in place. But, holding it in only one place wasn’t secure enough. I added a tab to hold the other end of the plastic.

Results

The key fits securely into the design

It’s been a few months, and the key holder seems to be working fine. This design wasn’t my first one. Compared to earlier iterations, this design performs better and holds the key much more securely. The face of the newly printed part replaces where the buttons used to be. The plastic is thin enough to be pressed down easily. At the buttons, I added small circular nubs to make pressing them even easier.

I enjoyed learning about wireframe modeling and prototyping. I’m not sure that my design is better than buying a low-cost replacement housing, but the experience made the process worthwhile.
